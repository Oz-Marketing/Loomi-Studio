## 1. The three numbers to lock before you call any contractor

Most "over budget" stories start here — fuzzy numbers at the start.

- **All-in project budget**, not just construction. Include design, permits,
  equipment, IT/low-voltage, furniture, signage, and a 10–15% contingency.
  The construction bid is often only ~60–70% of what you actually spend.
- **Realistic timeline**, including design and permitting — which in many Utah
  municipalities takes longer than the build itself. (More on this below.)
- **The cost of downtime.** If you'll lose chair time during construction, put a
  dollar figure on it. It changes whether you phase the work, build after hours,
  or move temporarily.

> **Rule of thumb:** know these three before you fall in love with a space or a
> floor plan. They quietly decide everything that follows.

## 2. Operatory & equipment rough-ins you can't cheaply change later

The expensive mistakes are the ones buried in the walls and floor.

- **Plumbing, air, vacuum, and electrical rough-ins** are set early and costly to
  move. Lock your operatory layout and equipment list *before* framing.
- **Equipment specs drive the building.** Chairs, imaging, sterilizers, and
  mechanical needs vary by manufacturer. Pick equipment early so rough-ins are
  right the first time.
- **Plan for the practice you'll have in 5 years**, not just today — leave
  capacity for an added op or upgraded imaging.

## 3. Imaging, plumbing & electrical surprises in older Utah buildings

Lease build-outs and older buildings hide costs that only surface once walls open.

- **Imaging (pan/ceph, CBCT)** needs proper power and, depending on the unit,
  shielding — easy to underestimate if specified late.
- **Aging electrical service** may not support modern equipment loads without an
  upgrade.
- **Plumbing and ADA** in older buildings often need more rework than expected to
  meet current code.

> An honest pre-construction walkthrough catches most of these before they become
> change orders. Insist on one.

## 4. How to keep seeing patients while you build

For an operating practice, this is the whole game. Options, roughly:

- **Phased construction** — work in zones so part of the practice stays open.
- **After-hours / weekend work** — slower and pricier, but minimal disruption.
- **Temporary space or reduced schedule** — sometimes the cheapest path overall
  once you price in lost chair time.

The right answer depends on your layout and schedule — but it should be decided
*before* the contract, not improvised mid-project. A contractor who's done live
healthcare environments will build the phasing plan with you.

## 5. Remodel vs. expand vs. relocate — how to choose

| If you… | Lean toward |
|---------|-------------|
| Like your location, need a refresh or better flow | **Remodel** |
| Are out of operatories but have adjacent space/land | **Expand** |
| Have outgrown the building or its systems | **Relocate / new build** |

Run the all-in numbers on each (Section 1). A remodel that fights an old building's
bones can cost more than a clean build-out elsewhere.

## 6. A short pre-construction checklist

- [ ] All-in budget with 10–15% contingency
- [ ] Equipment selected and specs in hand
- [ ] Operatory count and layout locked
- [ ] Imaging power/shielding confirmed
- [ ] Permitting timeline understood for your city
- [ ] Phasing / downtime plan agreed
- [ ] Contractor with healthcare experience and references
